# How We Work

We work async-first because thoughtful work rarely happens on demand in a crowded calendar. The default is to write things down, share context clearly, and let people respond when they can add signal instead of noise. That means decisions, updates, blockers, and ideas should live in durable places where anyone can find them later. Async communication is not about being slow. It is about reducing interruption, protecting focus, and making the work easier to follow without requiring everyone to be in the same room at the same time.

We also operate with a bias for action. If the next step is obvious, take it. If something is broken, fix it or propose the fix with a clear recommendation. We do not wait for perfect certainty before moving, and we do not confuse motion with progress. Action matters when it is tied to judgment. Make the best call you can with the information available, show your reasoning, and keep momentum. Small decisions made quickly and documented well are usually better than long delays dressed up as caution.

We avoid unnecessary meetings. Meetings are expensive because they pull multiple people out of focused work at once. A meeting should exist only when live discussion will materially improve the outcome: resolving a decision that is genuinely blocked, handling a sensitive conversation, or working through complexity faster together than apart. Everything else should happen in writing. If a meeting is needed, keep it short, send context in advance, and end with a clear owner, decision, and next step. If those things are missing, the meeting probably should not have happened.

How we communicate matters as much as what we communicate. Be direct, clear, and respectful. Say what is true, not what sounds polished. Surface risks early. Ask precise questions. When giving feedback, anchor it in the work, not the person. When asking for help, include enough context that someone can actually help without starting from zero. Good communication lowers coordination cost. It reduces rework, shortens handoff time, and makes trust easier to build. Silence, vagueness, and performative agreement do the opposite.

We make decisions with the goal of clarity, speed, and accountability. Start by identifying the owner. The owner gathers input, makes the call, and records the reasoning. Not every decision needs consensus, and treating every choice like a committee exercise slows the team down. The scale of the decision should match the scale of the process. Reversible decisions should be made quickly. Higher-impact decisions deserve more scrutiny, but still need a deadline and a decider. Once a decision is made, we commit to it long enough to learn from it, and we change course fast if the evidence says we should.

The standard underneath all of this is simple: communicate clearly, move with intent, and respect other people's time and attention. Async-first is how we protect focus. Bias for action is how we keep momentum. Fewer meetings is how we stay disciplined. Clear communication is how we stay aligned. Clear ownership is how we make better decisions. When these habits are working together, the team moves faster with less drama and better judgment.
