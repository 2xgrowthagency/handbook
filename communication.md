# Communication

## Where things live

**Basecamp** — client projects, deliverables, status updates, decisions. If it involves a client, it goes here.

**Linear** — internal tasks and issues. If something needs to get done and isn't a client deliverable, it goes here.

**Slack** — quick back-and-forth, time-sensitive questions, things that don't need to be permanent. Don't put anything important in Slack that you'd want someone to find later.

**Email** — client communication that needs a paper trail, or anything formal.

When in doubt, write it in the place where the relevant people will actually see it, and where it can be found again later.

## Response times

We respond to Slack messages the same business day. For client messages, we respond within one business day unless we've communicated otherwise.

If you need more time to give a good answer, acknowledge the message quickly, say when you'll follow up, and follow up when you said you would.

Urgent things should be labeled as urgent. Don't make people guess.

## Writing updates

A useful update covers: what happened, what it means, what's next, and who owns the next step. It does not require the reader to already know the context or to ask follow-up questions to understand it.

If you're updating a client on performance, lead with the most important thing — not a wall of numbers. If performance is down, say so early. Don't bury it.

## Giving feedback

Give feedback on the work, not the person. Be specific about what's off and what you'd want instead. Vague feedback ("this doesn't feel right") is worse than no feedback, because it creates work without direction.

If you have feedback on how something is being done — a process, a workflow, how a client relationship is being managed — say it directly to the person involved. If it's not getting addressed, bring it to John or Jenn.

## Raising problems

Surface problems early. A problem raised when it's small is a conversation. A problem raised when it's big is a crisis.

If you're unsure whether something is a real problem or not, raise it anyway. The cost of raising a non-issue is low. The cost of sitting on a real one is high.
