# Communication

We treat communication as part of the work, not admin around the work. Clients should not have to guess where an update lives, whether something is blocked, or how urgent an issue really is. Clear communication keeps projects moving, protects trust, and makes it easier to solve problems before they turn into surprises. The standard is simple: be direct, be timely, and leave a written record that someone else can follow later.

How we communicate starts with choosing the right channel. Email is for formal updates, decisions that need a durable record, and anything that may need to be forwarded internally. Slack is for day-to-day coordination, quick questions, and lightweight back-and-forth that helps unblock work. Shared docs are for plans, briefs, feedback, approvals, and anything that benefits from comments in context. Meetings are for decisions that genuinely need live discussion, sensitive conversations, or issues that would take longer to untangle in writing than to resolve together in twenty minutes.

Response times should be clear enough to reduce anxiety without creating fake urgency. During the workweek, we aim to respond to Slack messages within four business hours and to email within one business day. If something needs more investigation, we still acknowledge it quickly, confirm who owns the next step, and say when a fuller answer is coming. Urgent issues should be labeled clearly instead of being buried in a long thread. If something is truly time-sensitive, say that directly, explain the impact, and use the agreed urgent channel rather than hoping someone notices a message.

Client updates should make it easy to understand what happened, what matters, and what comes next. A useful update includes progress since the last check-in, open risks or blockers, decisions that need input, and the immediate next steps. We do not hide bad news behind soft language or wait for a perfect fix before raising a concern. If performance is down, a deadline is at risk, or an assumption has changed, we say so early and pair the issue with our recommendation.

Feedback works best when it is specific, timely, and tied to the work itself. If a client has feedback on creative, strategy, reporting, or process, the best approach is to leave it in the document, thread, or asset where the team can see exactly what needs to change. Vague feedback slows things down because it creates interpretation work before actual revision work can start. Useful feedback says what is off, why it matters, and what outcome the client wants instead. We will do the same in return when we are giving feedback on approvals, inputs, or internal client processes that affect the work.

Concerns should be raised early, directly, and at the lowest level that can solve them. If something feels unclear, off-track, or uncomfortable, bring it up with the primary point of contact first so it can be resolved quickly. If the issue involves responsiveness, quality, missed expectations, or a process breakdown, name the concern plainly and include examples. If the concern is sensitive, interpersonal, or not getting resolved through the normal path, escalate it to agency leadership. The goal is not drama or blame. The goal is to surface the problem, agree on the fix, and restore trust fast.
